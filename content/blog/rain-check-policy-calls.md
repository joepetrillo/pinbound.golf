Weather does not add phone calls one at a time. It creates a wave.

A cold morning can prompt dozens of versions of the same question: Is there a frost delay? How far behind is the first tee? Should the 8:10 group still arrive on time? After heavy rain, the questions shift to carts, closures, range conditions, and rain checks.

The people best equipped to decide—the superintendent, golf professional, and operations team—are also busy inspecting the property and adjusting the day. The communication system should turn their decision into many consistent answers without asking them to repeat it caller by caller.

## A frost delay is a turf decision, not a guess at the air temperature

The USGA explains that traffic on frost-covered grass can rupture plant cells and cause damage that [may take more than a month to recover](https://rulesworkshops.usga.org/content/usga/home-page/articles/2023/02/Golf-Front-Delay-USGA.html). Closely mown areas are especially vulnerable; the USGA estimates that a foursome takes 300 or more steps on a typical green.

Conditions can also vary across the property. Frost may linger on north-facing slopes, low areas, and shaded ground after the clubhouse thermometer rises. Even after the frost clears, maintenance teams may need time to finish setup.

That makes “we should open around 9” a risky phone answer unless the authorized course team has actually published it. A useful update distinguishes among:

- the course's current status;
- the estimated delay, if one has been approved;
- whether golfers should arrive at their original time;
- how tee times will be sequenced; and
- when the next update will be available.

## Rain creates several decisions, not one

“Is the course open?” is only the first branch. Wet weather can leave bunkers washed out, limit mowing, restrict carts, close the range, or make only part of the facility unavailable. The USGA notes that [storm effects can disrupt playing conditions for days](https://www.usga.org/content/usga/home-page/articles/2019/07/summer-on-the-golf-course--what-to-expect.html), even after the rain ends.

A weather template should therefore use separate fields rather than one all-purpose status:

| Decision    | Example update                                   |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Course      | Open; 45-minute frost delay                      |
| Carts       | Cart path only                                   |
| Practice    | Range closed; putting green open                 |
| First tee   | Groups should check in at original time          |
| Rain checks | Standard policy applies; shop decides exceptions |
| Review time | Update again at 9:30 a.m.                        |

Keeping those decisions separate makes it possible to change the cart rule without accidentally changing the course status.

## Safety messages need a different standard

Lightning is not a customer-service exception. It is a safety procedure.

The National Weather Service advises organizers of outdoor sports to have a plan that clearly defines [when activity stops, where people shelter, when activity resumes, and who monitors conditions](https://www.weather.gov/safety/lightning-sports). If thunder is audible, the storm is generally within about ten miles. The NWS recommends waiting at least 30 minutes after the last thunder before resuming outdoor activity.

An automated phone system should communicate the course's decision, not decide when play is safe. The designated course official and the facility's lightning plan remain in charge.

## Rain-check language deserves precision

Rain checks are policies, but their application often depends on facts: who suspended play, how many holes were completed, what product was purchased, whether a deadline applies, and whether the golfer is asking about a general rule or a specific transaction.

A good phone answer can explain the published policy. It should not promise a specific credit when required facts or staff approval are missing. The clean handoff sounds like this in principle: explain the rule, collect the reservation details, and route any exception or account-specific determination to an authorized employee.

That protects the golfer from receiving a confident answer the shop later has to reverse.

## Yesterday's correct answer is today's risk

Temporary facts need expiration. Without it, a Friday cart restriction can survive into Saturday's calls, or a morning delay can still be repeated after play has returned to schedule.

Every temporary update should include:

1. **Scope:** which course, range, or amenity it affects.
2. **Effective time:** when callers should begin hearing it.
3. **Expiration or review time:** when it must disappear or be confirmed again.
4. **Owner:** who is allowed to publish or change it.
5. **Fallback:** what to say when no current decision exists.

The fallback should acknowledge uncertainty and offer a person or callback. It should never fill the gap with a weather forecast presented as an operating decision.

## Publish once, then keep it current

The best long-term workflow publishes the same approved status to the phone, website, booking notice, staff view, and—where practical—email or text. Even if those systems are not connected today, using one structured update as the source reduces drift.

Pinbound lets authorized staff schedule temporary conditions with start and expiration times, then uses that approved message on calls. During a weather disruption, consistency is part of service—and uncertainty should be stated honestly.
