description: The phone rings when the pro shop is busiest. Industry research helps explain why—and what a better operating model looks like.

At 7:52 on a Saturday morning, the golf shop is not quiet. One group is checking in, another is asking about the cart rule, a single is hoping to get out early, and the starter needs an answer. Then the phone rings.

If the employee behind the counter helps the people standing in front of them, that is not poor service. It is a sensible decision with an unfortunate side effect: the caller waits, reaches voicemail, or tries again later.

This is why the missed-call problem is easy to underestimate. It looks like a staffing lapse when it is often a capacity mismatch. The phone tends to demand attention at the same moments the course does.

## The industry is busier, but the operating model is familiar

The pressure is not only anecdotal. The National Golf Foundation reports that [29.1 million Americans played on a golf course in 2025](https://www.ngf.org/the-clubhouse/golf-industry-research/), the eighth consecutive year of growth in on-course participation. The industry also set another record for rounds played with roughly 2,000 fewer facilities than it had at golf's participation peak two decades ago.

More play does not translate neatly into more capacity behind every counter. A golf shop still has concentrated arrival waves, weather interruptions, shift changes, and event-day demands. Unlike a conventional contact center, it cannot move every available employee onto the phone when call volume rises; those same people are running the physical operation.

NGF tried to quantify the result. Its 2025 analysis estimated that U.S. golf facilities spent [more than 6 million staff hours handling phone calls in one year](https://www.ngf.org/short-game/golfs-100-million-phone-problem/), representing more than $100 million in labor under its model. The more useful finding is that two-thirds of the calls in its research concerned reservations and pricing.

In other words, much of the volume is important without necessarily being complicated.

## Why online booking has not ended the phone call

It is tempting to see every reservation call as a failure to adopt online booking. The evidence points to a more complicated picture. NGF found that only 40% of golfers book tee times exclusively or mostly online, compared with 80–90% for flights, hotels, and rental cars.

Golf reservations also carry questions that a grid of available times may not answer:

- Is the quoted rate for walking or riding?
- Can a junior join this group?
- Are carts restricted today?
- Which course is the reservation on?
- Can the shop move an existing booking rather than create a new one?
- Does a same-day cancellation qualify for a rain check?

Publishing clearer information online should reduce avoidable calls, and every course should do it. But the goal should not be to force every golfer into one channel. A golfer may be driving, coordinating a group, unfamiliar with the facility, or trying to resolve an exception. The phone remains useful because conversation is flexible.

## A missed call is not automatically a lost foursome

Marketing claims about missed calls often jump straight from one unanswered ring to hundreds of dollars in lost revenue. That is too confident. Some callers try again. Some only want directions. Some would not have booked at any price.

The operational cost is real even when the revenue is unknowable:

1. **The interruption cost.** Answering breaks the employee away from check-in, retail, or another guest.
2. **The repeat-work cost.** Voicemail turns a live request into a message, a callback, and often another round of phone tag.
3. **The uncertainty cost.** The course cannot tell whether an abandoned call was a booking, a weather question, or a valuable event inquiry.
4. **The experience cost.** The caller does not know whether to wait, call again, or make another plan.

That is enough reason to improve the system without inventing a revenue number.

## Measure the conflict at your course

Compare phone activity with the daily operating rhythm. Review a few representative weeks and track:

| Measure | What it reveals |
| --- | --- |
| Calls by 15- or 30-minute interval | Whether demand clusters around opening, tee-time waves, lunch, or closing |
| Answered, abandoned, and voicemail calls | How often callers receive a useful first response |
| Call reason | Which work can be answered from information, which needs the tee sheet, and which needs judgment |
| Repeat calls from the same number | Whether unresolved calls are creating more volume |
| Transfers and callbacks | How much work is deferred rather than completed |
| Weather and event tags | Which spikes are predictable even if they are not regular |

Average daily call volume alone hides the problem. Twenty evenly distributed calls are different from twenty calls arriving during the first three tee-time waves.

## The better goal is not “answer everything with AI”

A useful call-handling system separates work by consequence.

Routine facts can come from course-approved information. Availability can come from the live tee sheet. A permitted reservation action can follow the course's rules and require a confirmed result before anyone promises success. Complaints, outing negotiations, identity questions, and explicit requests for a person can go directly to staff.

That is the model behind Pinbound: give the caller a useful path while the golf-shop team stays with the golfer in front of them. The measure of success is not how many people can be removed from the loop. It is whether routine demand stops competing with the moments where hospitality matters most.
